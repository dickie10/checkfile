Big Data - and why they matter

Big Data is not only about size, actually; more problems are associated with them than is commonly known. A leading database researcher, Michael Stonebraker, and IBM have summarized the problems as the four big Vs:

  • Volume: the sheer amount of bytes; for example, the European Space Agency plans to hold 10^12 satellite images under their custody, that is 1,000,000,000,000 images, each single of them hundreds of Megabytes.
  • Velocity: While our archives are already full, new data keep constantly pouring in over us. The NASA MODIS instrument delivers 1 Terabyte per day, the EOSDIS instrument even 5 TB per day. Researchers paraphrase this as "drinking from the firehose".
  • Variety: It is not simply satellite images, for example; we find 1-D timeseries, 2-D satellite imagery, 3-D x/y/t image timeseries and x/y/z geological data, and 4-D x/y/z/t cliamte and ocean data. But this is only the tip of the iceberg - naturally, easurements and observations are highly diverse, and integrating them for a unified, understandable picture is a big challenge.
  • Veracity: Any measurement or forecast is inexact, or maybe even plainly wrong. Therefore, each data item must be labeled with a quality indicator so that we know what risks we incur when using them - sometimes inaccurate data may serve well for a quick first glance, sometimes we need very exact data.

This is recognized by scientists worldwide:
